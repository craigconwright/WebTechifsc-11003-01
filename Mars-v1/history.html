Mission History

NASA's Mars Rover program represents humanity's most ambitious effort to explore another planet. Each mission has built upon previous successes, pushing the boundaries of technology and our understanding of Mars.

Curiosity Rover

Mission Overview

Curiosity is a car-sized rover designed to explore Gale Crater on Mars as part of NASA's Mars Science Laboratory (MSL) mission. The rover's main goal is to determine whether Mars ever had conditions suitable for microbial life.

Launch and Landing

Launch: November 26, 2011 from Cape Canaveral, Florida

Landing: August 6, 2012 at Gale Crater using the innovative "Sky Crane" landing system

Mission Duration: Originally planned for 2 years, still operating after 12+ years

Major Discoveries

  • Found evidence that Mars once had conditions suitable for microbial life
  • Discovered ancient streambed where water once flowed
  • Detected organic molecules in rock samples
  • Measured radiation levels to help plan future human missions
  • Confirmed the presence of methane in Mars' atmosphere

Key Technical Features

  • Weight: 1,982 pounds (899 kilograms)
  • Power: Nuclear radioisotope thermoelectric generator
  • Instruments: 17 cameras and a suite of advanced scientific tools
  • ChemCam laser can vaporize rocks from up to 23 feet away
  • Can drill into rocks and analyze samples

Perseverance Rover

Mission Overview

Perseverance is NASA's most advanced Mars rover to date. Its primary mission is to seek signs of ancient microbial life and collect rock and soil samples for potential return to Earth. The rover also includes the Ingenuity helicopter, the first aircraft to achieve powered flight on another planet.

Launch and Landing

Launch: July 30, 2020 from Cape Canaveral, Florida

Landing: February 18, 2021 at Jezero Crater

Mission Duration: At least one Mars year (about 687 Earth days)

Major Discoveries

  • Confirmed Jezero Crater once held a large lake and river delta
  • Collected rock samples showing signs of water interaction
  • Produced oxygen from Mars' carbon dioxide atmosphere (MOXIE experiment)
  • Ingenuity helicopter completed numerous successful flights
  • Discovered organic molecules in ancient rocks

Key Technical Features

  • Weight: 2,260 pounds (1,025 kilograms)
  • Power: Nuclear radioisotope thermoelectric generator
  • Instruments: 23 cameras and advanced scientific tools
  • Sample caching system to store Mars samples for future retrieval
  • MOXIE: Produces oxygen from Mars' atmosphere
  • Includes Ingenuity Mars Helicopter

Opportunity Rover

Mission Overview

Opportunity was one of two rovers sent to Mars in 2003 as part of the Mars Exploration Rover program. Originally designed for a 90-day mission, Opportunity exceeded all expectations by operating for nearly 15 years, becoming one of the most successful space missions in history.

Launch and Landing

Launch: July 7, 2003 from Cape Canaveral, Florida

Landing: January 25, 2004 at Meridiani Planum

Mission Duration: Planned for 90 days, operated for 5,352 days (nearly 15 years)

End of Mission: June 10, 2018 (contact lost during global dust storm)

Major Discoveries

  • Found evidence that Mars once had water on its surface
  • Discovered hematite "blueberries" - mineral formations created by water
  • Analyzed clay minerals that form in the presence of water
  • Traveled more than 28 miles (45 kilometers) - a Mars distance record
  • Survived multiple Martian winters and dust storms

Key Technical Features

  • Weight: 384 pounds (174 kilograms)
  • Power: Solar panels
  • Instruments: 9 cameras and scientific instruments
  • Rock abrasion tool (RAT) to grind into rock surfaces
  • Top speed: 0.1 mph (0.18 km/h)
