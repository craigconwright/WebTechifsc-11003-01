Thoughts on Progressive Enhancement

Progressive enhancement is a way of building for the web that starts with a dependable, accessible core and then gently layers on visual polish and richer interactions as the browser and device allow. At the base, you deliver clean HTML so that content and key tasks work even on older phones, slow connections, or assistive technologies. From there, you add CSS for layout and styling, and only then introduce JavaScript for features like dynamic forms, live validation, or smooth navigation, always treating those extras as enhancements rather than requirements.

In today‚Äôs landscape‚Äîwhere users arrive from every kind of network, browser, and screen size‚Äîthis mindset matters more than ever. It keeps experiences fast and resilient, improves accessibility and SEO, and reduces the risk of a blank or broken page when a script fails, an ad blocker intervenes, or a network request times out. For teams, it encourages cleaner separation of concerns and makes projects easier to maintain, test, and evolve over time. For users, it simply feels respectful: they get a site that loads quickly, lets them do what they came to do, and quietly gets better when their device can support it. In that sense, progressive enhancement is less a nostalgic best practice and more a modern, people-first strategy for building durable web experiences that hold up well beyond the next framework trend, and keep serving people gracefully as the web keeps changing.
